![Onze-Lieve-Vrouw Geboortekerk, Nazareth]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/2025-12-25-manuel-nazaret.jpg)

On Christmas morning in 2025, my girlfriend’s family took me to Mass. I was genuinely glad to be there. We went with her mother, her grandfather, and the two of us, to **Onze-Lieve-Vrouw Geboortekerk** — the parish church in Nazareth, Belgium (Dorp 16, 9810 Nazareth-De Pinte) — red brick against a cold, bright winter sky, the cemetery laid out in front of it like dates on a calendar you’re not supposed to read too closely.

For a few years I’ve been trying to become a better version of myself, and part of that has been drawing nearer to faith even when the voices I admire most online don’t share it. There is still something unmistakable in prayer for me — an attempt to stand still long enough to be addressed by whatever is larger than my plans.

A mix of **Dostoevsky** and **Kierkegaard** nudged the door open; the rest has been practice, showing up for things I once thought impossible. I’m a competitive, sporty person — always chasing personal records — and I’d be lying if I said some of the calm I’ve found in prayer hasn’t helped me show up on the track and in ordinary days with a steadier heart.

I don’t pretend everything can be proven. I choose to believe anyway — especially within Christianity — because life already deals you a hand, and I’d rather love that hand than spend every Sunday arguing with it.

That morning the church did what churches do on Christmas: warmth, candles, a nativity scene acted out with a real baby, unbearably cute, the kind of detail that makes cynicism feel cheap.

Then, in the middle of it, the sound changed. Chairs scraping. Urgent voices. You know that knot in the stomach when a roomful of people realizes the same thing at once: *this is not part of the program.*

My girlfriend’s mother is an osteopath. She moved before I’d fully understood what I was seeing. A man lay on the floor. The liturgy stumbled forward for a little while — priests and people doing their best to hold a fragile kind of order — until the service had to stop. Paramedics arrived. Somewhere in the swirl, I caught the priest: calming gestures, *I’ve got this* written in his shoulders, the way a good captain steadies a deck. He looked like a cross between a monk and Santa — tall, broad, a magnificent red beard.

When we finally filed out, he caught my eye and lifted a flat hand toward his chest: *we’re okay. He’s in good hands.*

We waited outside for my girlfriend’s mother. I laughed once, without meaning to — the sort of laugh that’s really nerves — because of course *this* would be the Mass I attend with the whole family. Even her grandfather joked that he might skip the next invitation.

When her mother joined us, the story she carried was heavy. She had helped with CPR. From what she could tell in those moments, the man was gone — had been for a while.

We drove home quiet. I tried to stack the theology: Christmas, a child in a feeding trough, then *this* — as if the day itself was arguing with me.

The next morning she sent a link. An article. A statement from the man’s wife.

He was alive. Stable. Grateful. She thanked everyone in the church — the people who moved, who called, who knelt, who refused to look away. She said plainly that if it had happened with the two of them alone, she wouldn’t have managed. She wrote about believing in the church, in community, in showing up, with a force that shook me more than the emergency had.

I had braced for a tragedy. I got a plot twist I still don’t have clean language for.

And if you want the universe to feel *unreasonably literary*: the next time I went to church was in Uruguay. The reading that morning was **Lazarus** — stone rolled away, impossible hope dressed up as an old story.

Coincidence? Providence? I’ve stopped keeping score.

I know this much: I found a place in the Christian church — not because it always feels tidy, but because it makes room for mornings like that one in Nazareth, when the world presses in and something, or Someone, still insists on another chapter.
